Indians Supporting the War

On September 26, 1914, the Lahore Division arrived in Marseilles, France. The French initially mistook the Gorkhas for Japanese and referred to them as "Les Hindous. When the Indian soldiers arrived, the British Expeditionary Force was barely holding the Ypres Salient, a 38-mile stretch between the Belgians and the French, after being exhausted by the retreat from Mons to Marne and the advance to the Aisne, suffering 10,000 casualties and facing severe shortages in artillery.

In September and October 1914, the critical battles of the Marne and Ypres were pivotal in preventing a German victory. If the Germans had succeeded in their assaults, particularly along Menin Road or at Neuve Chapelle, they could have forced a British withdrawal from the continent. The Indian Army played a crucial role in saving the British Expeditionary Force. By December 1914, Princess Patricia's Canadian Light Infantry arrived in Europe, followed by the 1st Canadian Division, which landed in France on January 14, 1915.

Over 135,000 British Indian Army troops fought alongside Canadian forces on the Western Front in battles such as 2nd Ypres, St. Julian, Festubert, The Somme, and Vimy. If Indian troops from the Lahore Division hadn't arrived in Belgium in 1914, the Germans would have seized the ports, preventing the Canadian Expeditionary Force from landing on the European mainland. Guts, Glory, and Izzat

The Indian Army Corps, wearing tropical uniforms, endured one of the harshest winters on record while fighting in major battles across Europe from 1914 to 1916. Despite being in unfamiliar land, facing an unknown enemy, and fighting for a cause not their own, they served with honor for their country and regiments, embodying the spirit of "IZZAT" (honor). - Major Gordon Corrigan

"Izzat" was a key motivating factor for Indian soldiers during World War I, helping them overcome challenges that Western soldiers did not face. It is a complex concept that goes beyond just honor or face, encompassing both external and internal elements. Externally, it includes promotions, rank, medals, marriage, wealth, and land, all of which contribute to one's Izzat. Internally, it involves a sense of self-respect, duty, reputation, and pride. The concept of Izzat is woven into nearly every story from the war, influencing soldiers at personal, community, and regimental levels.

Europe Indian Army Corps

Indian Army Corps soldiers in World War I faced immense challenges, fighting in unfamiliar European lands and harsh, freezing winters without adequate training or proper uniforms until early 1915. They endured piecemeal deployments, language barriers with allied troops, and the horrors of artillery, shelling, and gas attacks.

Despite their bravery and daily risks for little more than pride, these heroes remained largely unrecognized after the war, neglected by the British Empire they served and overlooked by their homeland. Their sacrifices were further diminished by the British breaking their insincere promise of self-rule for India, leaving their contributions to the war effort undervalued.

Western Europe Front

In 1914, two critical battles, the Battle of Marne in September and the First Battle of Ypres in October, were pivotal for the Allies' survival. Had the German assaults been successful, the British Expeditionary Force (BEF) could have been forced to withdraw, potentially leading to a British defeat. The Indian Army played a crucial role in saving the BEF during these battles.

Indian soldiers captured by the Germans were treated better than those held by the Turks. The first mosque in Germany was even built for Indian POWs. Despite efforts by the Germans to encourage Indian soldiers to defect, only a few cases of defections to the enemy occurred. Additionally, over 300 POWs' voices were recorded and preserved by the Germans.

Indian Army Mobilization for the British

In August 1914, the Indian Army Corps, the only professional army the British could rely on, mobilized for Europe via Karachi and Mumbai. For many Punjabi, Garhwali, and Gurkha soldiers, this was their first time seeing the sea and crossing the "Kala Pani" (Black Water), a significant cultural taboo for many Hindu soldiers.

Princely States & British Indian Army

Princely States & British Indian Army By 1914, one-third of India was governed by over 700 princely states. Through the Imperial Service Scheme, these states were allowed to train and equip portions of their forces to British standards, making them available for operations within the Indian Army. Among the 27 states maintaining Imperial Service Contingents, 21 participated in World War I, primarily in the Middle East, while 4 contributed forces to France.

During World War I, Indian nationalists believed that their support for the British war effort would lead to Dominion Status, akin to that of the "White Commonwealth." Mahatma Gandhi, having returned from South Africa in 1915, supported the war effort as he had previously in the Boer War. Meanwhile, poet Rabindranath Tagore expressed skepticism about nationalism, noting India's struggles, saying, "We, the famished, ragged ragamuffins of the East are to win freedom for all humanity!" India endured severe hardships during the war, including high taxation, inflation, economic losses from disrupted trade, and a devastating influenza epidemic that took many lives.

Impact of Post 1857 First War of Independence

The reorganization of the military following the 1857 First War of Independence, also known as the "Sepoy Mutiny," penalized those who participated in the rebellion and rewarded those who remained loyal. Regiments that mutinied were disbanded, and the concept of "Martial Races" emerged, reshaping the composition of the Indian Army.

Before the mutiny, the Bengal Army predominantly consisted of plainsmen, including many Brahmins who had rebelled, unlike the Gurkhas who stayed loyal. This led to a shift in perception, with people from hilly regions or northern latitudes viewed as tougher, more honest, and "manlier" than those from the tropics or plains. Sikhs, although not hill-men, were favored for their northern origins and non-Hindu identity, alongside other communities in Punjab and the North.

These groups were considered more reliable, and the gaps left by disbanded regiments were filled by Gurkhas, Sikhs, and Punjabis. Under leaders like Roberts and Kitchener, the Indian Army was unified, with an increased proportion of soldiers drawn from the so-called Martial Races. Within 50 years, Punjab became the cornerstone of the Indian Army, with representation from Sikhs, Dogras, Jats, Punjabi Mussalmans, Pathans, Gurkhas, and Garhwalis. However, the Martial Races theory, while influential, was largely based on unscientific and biased assumptions.
