The Liberation
of Haifa

The Liberation of Haifa on Sept 23, 1918, was a decisive battle during WW1, where the British Indian cavalry of the Jodhpur and Mysore Lancers captured the city from the Ottoman and German forces, securing a key strategic port for the Allied advance in Palestine.

Main Content - Section 1

1918

Indian Cavalry in Haifa

On September 23, 1918, during World War I, the Indian Cavalry captured Haifa, ending 400 years of Ottoman rule. This victory enabled the local Christian and Jewish communities to flourish and contributed to the eventual formation of Israel. It also prevented the Ottomans from crucifying Abdu’l Baha on Mount Carmel and destroying the Baha'i faith shrines, while playing a crucial role in the broader success of Allenby’s forces in routing the Ottoman armies across Palestine, Lebanon, and Syria.

The 15th Imperial Service Cavalry Brigade, consisting of the Jodhpur Lancers, Mysore Lancers, and Hyderabad Lancers, was part of the 5th Cavalry Division of the Desert Mounted Corps. Tasked with capturing Haifa from Ottoman control, the brigade played a crucial role in the city's liberation. The victory came at a heavy cost, with 900 Indian soldiers sacrificing their lives. This battle marked a pivotal moment in World War I and the region’s future and is remembered as the last successful cavalry charge in military history.

Events During the Liberation

On September 22, 1918, a force of 700 Turkish soldiers from the Haifa garrison attempted to reach Tiberias but encountered the 13th Cavalry Brigade's outposts and were attacked by the 18th Lancers. Many were killed, and 311 were captured, along with four machine guns. An aerial reconnaissance later suggested Haifa had been evacuated. On the same day, a detachment of Light Armoured Cars, led by Brigadier General A. D'A. King advanced along the Nazareth Road to occupy Haifa. However, they encountered barricades and came under artillery and machine gun fire from Mount Carmel, forcing them to withdraw with minor casualties.

On September 23, 1918, the 14th and 15th Cavalry Brigades handed over their line to the 3rd Australian Light Horse Brigade and began their march on Haifa. The route was restricted by the boggy terrain of the River Kishon, and the 15th Cavalry Brigade came under fire from 77mm guns positioned on Mount Carmel. By midday, the 14th Cavalry Brigade, along with divisional headquarters, successfully occupied the Kishon Railway Bridge and Harosheth of the Gentiles.

The Jodhpur Lancers, supported by 'B' Battery H.A.C., launched an attack on Haifa, encountering strong resistance. Despite intense machine gun fire, they made a successful charge. Meanwhile, a squadron of Mysore Lancers, supported by Sherwood Rangers, maneuvered over Mount Carmel, captured two naval guns on the Carmel ridge, and charged the enemy’s machine gun positions.

British forces successfully defended the canal and later went on the offensive, crossing into the Sinai Peninsula before invading Palestine. Major Dalpat Singh Shekhawat

Major Dalpat Singh Shekhawat Thakur, commander of the Jodhpur Lancers, led the final successful cavalry charge in military history during the war. He was killed in action during this attack and is remembered as the "Hero of Haifa" for his pivotal role in the city's liberation. For his bravery, he was posthumously awarded the Military Cross.

Major Dalpat entrusted the critical task of capturing the defile to his most senior officer, Captain Aman Singh of 'B' Squadron. Following its capture, 'D' Squadron, led by Captain Anop Singh, was to advance through the defile and seize the machine guns positioned on the eastern mount. Securing the defile was the pivotal operation upon the success of the entire battle.

The Heroic Action of Major Dalpat

At 2 PM, Brigadier Harbord ordered the Jodhpur Lancers to advance from near Beled Esh Sheikh. Led by 'B' Squadron, they crossed the Acre railway line under heavy fire, suffering significant horse casualties. Lt. Col. Holden directed Maj. Dalpat to charge the machine guns on Mount Carmel’s lower slopes. During the maneuver, Maj. Dalpat was shot in the spine and later died from his wounds. A previous reconnaissance had been disrupted, and upon reaching the river, they found the embankment impassable due to quicksand.

At a critical moment, Captain Bahadur Aman Singh took command and led a fearless charge, eliminating over 30 enemy troops and capturing two machine guns and two camel guns. Their decisive assault secured the narrow defile, clearing the road to Haifa. The 'B' Squadron galloped across both branches of Wadi Ashlul el Wawy, charging into the enemy and securing the defile. This strategic breakthrough, via the Turkish bridge between Wadi Selman and the mountain, cleared the main road into Haifa for the regiment’s advance. After securing the defile, 'B' Squadron advanced along the lower slopes of Mount Carmel and stormed the German colony west of Haifa, seizing multiple machine guns and inflicting heavy losses on Turkish and German forces.

Main Content - Section 3

Location of Haifa

The city of Haifa, present-day Israel, located just north of Jerusalem on the Mediterranean coast, was strategically positioned between the steep slopes of Mount Carmel to the south and the Nahr el Muqatta (River Kishon) to the north. The narrow defile between the mountain and the river was heavily defended by Turkish artillery and gun emplacements.

'Abdu'l-Baha

‘Abdu’l-Baha, the son of the Founder of the Baha’i Faith and its spiritual leader, resided in Haifa, present-day Israel. The Baha’i Faith, which began in the Ottoman Empire in the 19th century, had grown in influence under ‘Abdu’l-Baha’s leadership and humanitarian efforts. Alarmed by his growing popularity, the Turkish commander-in-chief, Jamal Pasha, threatened to crucify ‘Abdu’l-Baha on Mount Carmel and destroy all Baha’i shrines and holy places.

When news of this threat reached the British community in Egypt and Europe, Major Tudor-Pole, a British army intelligence officer and follower of ‘Abdu’l-Baha, persuaded military authorities to intervene and protect him. With the liberation of Haifa in 1918, the danger to ‘Abdu’l-Baha’s life was finally lifted, preserving the Baha’i holy sites in Haifa and nearby Acre. Haifa had spiritual importance to the Bahá'í Faith, which had begun to grow within the Ottoman Empire. The spiritual leader, Abdu’l-Bahá, had attracted the Ottomans’ attention due to his humanitarian work, leading to a threat of his execution.

The Capture of Two Regiments

Together the two regiments capture: resulted in 1,350 German and Ottoman prisoners, including two German officers and 35 Ottoman officers. The Allies seized 17 artillery guns (four 4.2 guns, eight 77mm guns, four camel guns, and a 6-inch naval gun), along with 11 machine guns. Their own casualties included eight killed, 34 wounded, 60 horses killed, and 83 horses injured.

Under the Turkish Ottoman Rule

Under Turkish Ottoman rule since 1516, Haifa had a population of 24,634 according to the British census of 1922, with 9,377 Muslims, 8,863 Christians, 6,230 Jews, and 164 others. The city began to develop as an industrial port, with increased trade facilitated by the railway, attracting merchants. The construction of the Technion Institute of Technology, a Jewish technical school, began, and the Bahá'í Faith began to emerge in the region.

Generals of the Ottoman Army

General Otto Liman von Sanders (1855–1929) was the German commander responsible for modernizing the Ottoman Army. General Mustafa Kemal (1881–1938), later known as Atatürk, was a skilled career soldier who played a crucial role in the Ottoman success at Gallipoli in 1915. General Edward Allenby was also a key figure in the military operations of the time.
